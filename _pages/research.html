--- layout: single title: "Research" permalink: /research/ author_profile: true --- # Patrolling the Powerful: The Reputational Consequences of Punishing Power-Holders *Revise and Resubmit at the Journal of Experimental Psychology: General* Powerful leaders have a disproportionate capacity to harm others, yet their followers may be reluctant to confront their unethical conduct because doing so can expose them to significant risks of retaliation. Nevertheless, people do sometimes stand up to misbehaving leaders. In an archival study of more than 14,000 federal workers in the United States, we found that when they had witnessed their supervisors perpetrating a serious incident of workplace aggression or harassment in the previous two years, they confronted them nearly 25% of the time. Strikingly, these people were not the direct targets of the misconduct. Rather, they were bystanders who nevertheless chose to confront their bosses. In joint work with my postdoctoral advisor, Ena Inesi, we examine why people take such risks on behalf of others. We hypothesise that one important reason is reputational: people who confront unethical leaders earn strong reputations, which in turn yield meaningful social rewards, such as greater trust from their peers.
