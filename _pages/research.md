# Patrolling the Powerful: The Reputational Consequences of Punishing Power-Holders
Currently Revise and Resubmit at the *Journal of Experimental Psychology: General*

Powerful leaders have a disproportionate capacity to harm others, yet their followers may be reluctant to confront their unethical conduct because doing so can expose them to significant risks of retaliation. Nevertheless, people do sometimes stand up to harmful leaders. In an archival study of more than 14,000 federal workers in the United States, we found that when these employees had witnessed their supervisors perpetrating a serious incident of workplace aggression or harassment in the previous two years, they confronted them nearly 25% of the time. Strikingly, they were not the direct targets of this misconduct. Rather, they were bystanders who chose to confront their bosses on behalf of other victims. In joint work with my postdoctoral advisor, [Ena Inesi](https://www.london.edu/faculty-and-research/faculty-profiles/i/inesi-me/), we examine why people take such risks. We hypothesise that one important reason is reputational: people who confront unethical leaders in this way earn moral reputations, which yield meaningful social rewards. We investigate this reputational account across a series of experiments.

# Right to Brag? The Reputational Consequences of Disclosing Prosocial Behavior
Currently under review at the *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*

Research has shown that people's reputations suffer when tell others about their good deeds. Rather than seeing these individuals as being moral, we see them as self-promoters who are trying to reap benefits from advertising their moral character. However, what we know in this area is based on people bragging about unambiguously good deeds, like donating to charities. But good deeds come in more ambiguous forms. Imagine that you see a stranger attempting to steal someone else’s bicycle, then confront them. This is an example of a third-party punishment. Your behaviour may have been confrontational, but it was also prosocial—by intervening, you are protecting someone else's property and upholding social norms against theft. Another line of research has shown that punishers are seen as trustworthy, cooperative, and moral; but also dominant and aggressive. In other words, we infer mixed traits in these types of people. In joint work with my postdoctoral advisor, [Ena Inesi](https://www.london.edu/faculty-and-research/faculty-profiles/i/inesi-me), and [Jonathan Berman](https://www.london.edu/faculty-and-research/faculty-profiles/b/berman-j-z-1), we suggest that when people tell others about their punishments, their reputations often benefit. We don't see these individuals as self-promoters to the same extent, and we instead infer that they are trying to warn others about the threatening incidents that prompted their interventions (e.g., there being a bicycle thief on the loose). We explore this by examining motive-attributions in a series of experiments.
