# Patrolling the Powerful: The Reputational Consequences of Punishing Power-Holders
Currently Revise and Resubmit at the *Journal of Experimental Psychology: General*

Powerful leaders have a disproportionate capacity to harm others, yet their followers may be reluctant to confront their unethical conduct because doing so can expose them to significant risks of retaliation. Nevertheless, people do sometimes stand up to harmful leaders. In an archival study of more than 14,000 federal workers in the United States, we found that when these employees had witnessed their supervisors perpetrating a serious incident of workplace aggression or harassment in the previous two years, they confronted them nearly 25% of the time. Strikingly, they were not the direct targets of these misconduct incidents. Rather, they were bystanders who chose to confront their bosses on behalf of other victims. In joint work with my postdoctoral advisor, [Ena Inesi](https://www.london.edu/faculty-and-research/faculty-profiles/i/inesi-me/), we examine why people take such risks. We hypothesise that one important reason is reputational: people who confront unethical leaders in this way earn moral reputations, which yield rewards. We investigate this reputational account across a series of experiments.

# Right to Brag? The Reputational Consequences of Disclosing Prosocial Behavior
Currently under review at the *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*

Research has shown that people's reputations suffer when they tell others about their good deeds. Rather than seeing these individuals as being moral, we see them as self-promoters who are trying to reap benefits by advertising their good deeds. However, what we know in this area is based on the effects of people bragging about unambiguously good deeds, like donating to charity. But good deeds come in more ambiguous forms. Imagine that you see a stranger attempting to steal someone else’s bicycle, then confront them. This is an example of a third-party punishment. Your behaviour may have been confrontational, but it was also prosocial—by intervening, you protected someone else's property and upheld social norms against theft. Research has shown that these kinds of punishers are seen as trustworthy, cooperative, and moral; but also dominant and aggressive. In other words, we infer mixed traits in these types of people. In joint work with my postdoctoral advisor, [Ena Inesi](https://www.london.edu/faculty-and-research/faculty-profiles/i/inesi-me), and [Jonathan Berman](https://www.london.edu/faculty-and-research/faculty-profiles/b/berman-j-z-1), we suggest that when people tell others about their punishments, their reputations often benefit rather than suffer. We don't see these individuals as self-promoters; instead we infer that they are simply trying to warn others about the threatening incidents that prompted their interventions (e.g., there being a bicycle thief on the loose). We explore this by examining motive-attributions from bragging about punishment in a series of experiments.

# More Than a Consensus? A Social Relations Model Analysis of Status
Currently under review at *Personality and Social Psychology Bulletin*

In social psychology, status is defined as the relative extent to which individuals are respected and admired in social groups. Status plays a pivotal role in social interactions: for instance, it drives the opportunities people get to participate in decisions and exert influence over others. Decades of research have studied why some people earn more status than others. This work has shown that groups confer status on those who are seen to be competent and commited to advancing the collective interest. However, this work has also underplayed how subjective these perceptions are. In fact, there are many instances of social groups where people are seen as highly competent by some, but incompetent by others. A big reason for this is that relationships are nested in groups; and our perceptions of status are heavily influenced by our experiences in these relationships. In this project, together with my PhD advisors [Sebastien Brion](https://www.iese.edu/faculty-research/faculty/sebastien-brion/) and [Anneloes Raes](https://www.iese.edu/faculty-research/faculty/anneloes-raes/), we wanted to show just how much relationships impact status conferral. Using a statistical method called the Social Relations Model, we showed that the nuanced status perceptions that emerge in relationships play a critical role in explaining who gains status. We also explored which relationships contribute disproportionately to this effect, and found that status rivals tend to avoid these relationship effects. 

### chat version
In social psychology, status refers to how much respect and admiration a person receives relative to others in a group. Status shapes everyday interactions: it affects who gets heard, who is included in decisions, and whose ideas carry influence. Decades of research have therefore asked why some people gain more status than others. A common finding is that groups give status to people they see as competent and committed to the group’s success.

But these judgments are not always shared. The same person may appear highly capable to one teammate and far less capable to another. This is because status is not formed only through a group-wide consensus; it is also shaped by particular relationships. What one person values, notices, and experiences in another can influence the respect and admiration they give that person.

In this project, conducted with my PhD advisors Sebastien Brion and Anneloes Raes, we examined just how much these relationship-specific judgments matter. We used the Social Relations Model, a statistical method that separates perceptions shared across a group from perceptions that are unique to particular pairs of people. Across three samples, we found that a substantial share of the variation in status judgments was relationship-specific. In other words, the status someone receives depends not only on who that person is, but also on who is evaluating them.

We also asked when these relationship-specific judgments are strongest. They were larger when two people were farther apart in the group’s status hierarchy and smaller when they occupied similar positions. One possible explanation is that people are more cautious about departing from the group consensus when evaluating a close status rival, because doing so could provoke conflict or alter their relative standing. More broadly, the findings suggest that status is not simply a group-wide consensus: it is also built, one relationship at a time.
