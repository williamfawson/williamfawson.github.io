# Patrolling the Powerful: The Reputational Consequences of Punishing Power-Holders

Currently Revise and Resubmit at the *Journal of Experimental Psychology: General*

Powerful leaders have a disproportionate capacity to harm others, yet their followers may be reluctant to confront their unethical conduct because doing so can expose them to significant risks of retaliation. Nevertheless, people do sometimes stand up to harmful leaders. In an archival study of more than 14,000 federal workers in the United States, we found that when these employees had witnessed their supervisors perpetrating a serious incident of workplace aggression or harassment in the previous two years, they confronted them nearly 25% of the time. Strikingly, they were not the direct targets of this misconduct. Rather, they were bystanders who chose to confront their bosses on behalf of other victims. In joint work with my postdoctoral advisor, Ena Inesi, we examine why people take such risks. We hypothesise that one important reason is reputational: people who confront unethical leaders in this way earn moral reputations, which yield meaningful social rewards. We investigate this reputational account across a series of experiments.
